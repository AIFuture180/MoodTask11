You're Feeling Sad

Lift your spirits with these science-backed activities:

  • Gratitude Practice: Reflect on positive moments.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Andrew Huberman): Gratitude activates the prefrontal cortex, regulating emotional processing and reducing sadness, as Huberman notes in his work on neural plasticity.
  • Soundscape: Listen to calming ocean waves.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Matt Walker): Nature sounds lower amygdala activity, calming emotional distress and promoting relaxation, per Walker’s research on auditory stimuli and sleep.
  • Breathing Exercise: Try slow, deep breaths.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Andrew Huberman): Deep breathing stimulates the vagus nerve, reducing sadness by activating the parasympathetic nervous system, as Huberman explains.

You're Feeling Angry

Channel your energy with these activities:

  • Punching Bag: Release tension with a virtual punching bag.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Andrew Huberman): Physical activity reduces cortisol and adrenaline, dissipating anger. Huberman emphasizes that exertion calms the amygdala, restoring emotional balance.
  • Breathing Exercise: Practice 4-7-8 breathing.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Lisa Feldman Barrett): Slow breathing shifts emotional states by altering physiological arousal, as Barrett’s theory of constructed emotion suggests.
  • Worry Shredder: Write and shred your frustrations.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Andrew Huberman): Expressive writing externalizes anger, reducing amygdala activity and promoting clarity, per Huberman’s stress research.

You're Feeling Anxious

  • Color Focus: Ground yourself with a color focus exercise.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Andrew Huberman): Visual focus diverts attention from anxious thoughts, stabilizing heart rate by engaging the occipital lobe, per Huberman’s work.
  • Soundscape: Listen to soothing rain sounds.
    • Why it helps (Dr. Matt Walker): Nature sounds reduce sympathetic nervous system activity, easing anxiety and promoting relaxation, as Walker’s sleep studies show.
